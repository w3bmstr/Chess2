While we cannot mathematically "solve" chess to force a draw from move 1 yet (like in Checkers), we have "practically" solved it. Top Grandmasters and engines use specific strategies to make it nearly impossible for the opponent to win.

If your goal is to "hack" chess and force a draw every game, you must adopt the philosophy of "The Dullard." You stop trying to win; you try to kill the game.

Here is your "Forced Draw" Repertoire for White and Black.

Phase 1: The White Repertoire (The "Vacuum Cleaner")
As White, you have the first move advantage. To force a draw, you use that advantage not to attack, but to trade pieces immediately and simplify the position.

1. The Exchange Variations If Black plays a solid defense, you immediately trade pawns to open lines and exchange pieces. This removes the "tension" from the board.

Against the French Defense (1. e4 e6): Play the Exchange Variation (2. d4 d5 3. exd5 exd5). The position becomes perfectly symmetrical. It is famously difficult for Black to create winning chances here because there are no imbalances.

Against the Caro-Kann (1. e4 c6): Play the Exchange Variation (2. d4 d5 3. exd5 cxd5). Again, symmetry is the enemy of decisive results.

Against the Slav (1. d4 d5): Play the Exchange Slav (1. d4 d5 2. c4 c6 3. cxd5). This is widely hated by aggressive players because it sucks the life out of the game instantly.

2. The London System (1. d4 and 2. Bf4) This is a "system" opening where you play the same setup almost regardless of what Black does. It is rock solid. You build a pyramid of pawns, develop your pieces safely, and refuse to take risks. It is very hard for Black to crack this wall if you don't overextend.

Phase 2: The Black Repertoire (The "Berlin Wall")
As Black, you are defending. To force a draw, you need openings that are "bulletproof."

1. The Berlin Defense (vs. 1. e4) This is the single most famous "drawing weapon" in chess history.

The Moves: 1. e4 e5 2. Nf3 Nc6 3. Bb5 Nf6

Why it works: It forces the "Berlin Endgame" where Queens are traded off very early (often by move 9). Without Queens on the board, White rarely has enough firepower to checkmate you.

History: Vladimir Kramnik used this to defeat Garry Kasparov in 2000. Kasparov, the greatest attacker in history, could not break down the "Berlin Wall" and didn't win a single game.

2. The Petroff Defense (vs. 1. e4)

The Moves: 1. e4 e5 2. Nf3 Nf6

Why it works: Instead of defending your pawn, you counter-attack White's pawn. This usually leads to early trades and a symmetrical pawn structure.

Reputation: It is known as the "Draw Weapon" of top GMs. If a Grandmaster needs a draw to win a tournament, they almost always play the Petroff.

3. The Queen's Gambit Declined (vs. 1. d4)

The Strategy: Play solid, classical chess. Do not accept gambits. Simply defend your center (1. d4 d5 2. c4 e6). If White tries to attack, you trade pieces to ease the pressure.

Phase 3: The "Drawing" Tactics
Once you are in the game, you use these tactical concepts to force the result:

1. Liquidate into Opposite-Colored Bishops If you reach an endgame where you have a Light-Squared Bishop and your opponent has a Dark-Squared Bishop (and you both have pawns), it is almost a forced draw.

The Reason: You put your pawns on dark squares (so their bishop can't touch them) and your King on light squares. You can block their pawns forever. Neither side can attack the other effectively.

2. The Fortress If you are losing, don't resign. Build a "Fortress." This is a setup where your opponent has more material (like a Queen vs. your Rook), but they cannot break in.

Example: You tuck your King in the corner and shield it with a Rook and pawns such that the enemy King cannot approach without getting checked away.

3. The Perpetual Check Always look for a sacrifice that exposes the enemy King, not to kill it, but to check it forever. The Queen is the best piece for this. If you check the King, and it moves, and you check it again, and it moves back... you have forced a draw by Threefold Repetition.

Summary of the "Forced Draw" Playstyle
Avoid Complexity: Never enter "sharp" positions where one mistake loses the game.

Symmetry is King: Copy the opponent's pawn structure. If they have a pawn on e4, you want one on e5.

Trade Queens: The moment you see a chance to trade Queens without losing material, do it. Winning without Queens is incredibly hard.
